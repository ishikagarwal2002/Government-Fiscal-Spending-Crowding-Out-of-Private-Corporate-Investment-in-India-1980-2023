
# Government Fiscal Spending and Private Corporate Investment in India (1980–2023)

##  Overview
This research project examines the relationship between government fiscal activity and private corporate investment in India over the period 1980–2023. The central objective is to evaluate whether fiscal expansion leads to **crowding-out** or **crowding-in** of private investment, and how this relationship evolves across different policy regimes.

The study extends existing literature by incorporating a longer time horizon, structural breaks, and reform-specific dynamics to better capture India's evolving macroeconomic environment.

##  Methodology
The analysis employs advanced time-series econometric techniques:

- **ARDL (Autoregressive Distributed Lag) Bounds Testing Approach**
  - To estimate long-run and short-run relationships
  - Suitable for variables with mixed integration orders (I(0)/I(1))

- **Dynamic OLS / ADL Framework**
  - Used as a robustness check
  - Helps validate long-run coefficients without strict cointegration requirements

- Incorporation of:
  - Structural break (FRBM Act, 2003)
  - Reform interaction terms
  - Macroeconomic control variables (credit flow, inflation, vulnerability, etc.)

  - Fiscal deficit
  - Credit flow
  - Lending rate, inflation, output gap, trade openness

##  Key Findings
- **Fiscal Deficit Crowding-Out**:  
  A 1% increase in fiscal deficit reduces private investment by approximately **0.4–0.5%** in the long run.

- **Role of Public Investment**:  
  Public investment does not have a significant standalone effect, but becomes **complementary (crowding-in)** in the post-FRBM reform period.

- **Credit as a Key Driver**:  
  Availability of bank credit emerges as the **strongest short-run determinant** of private investment.

- **Regime-Dependent Dynamics**:  
  The relationship between public and private investment is **not stable across time** and becomes well-defined only after accounting for structural reforms.

---

##  Policy Implications
- Importance of **fiscal discipline** to avoid crowding-out effects  
- Need to prioritize **infrastructure spending** for crowding-in benefits  
- Strengthening **financial intermediation and credit availability** is crucial for boosting private investment
